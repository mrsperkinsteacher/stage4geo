Perspective-taking — 10 minutes, essential for Part D
Specific group activity

Students write one sentence: "For [group], Point Clare is more/less liveable because..."

Groups to choose from: elderly residents, young families, teenagers, people with disabilities, low-income renters, commuters to Gosford/Sydney.

⚡ Part B — Priority
Why this lesson exists

Part B is worth 10 marks. Students must find real data, validate the source, draw a column graph with BOLTSS, and write 3–4 sentences of interpretation. Every sub-skill is taught here. Nothing in this lesson is optional.

Step 1 — The websites. Where to find the data.

These are the four approved sources. Walk through ABS QuickStats on the board first — it is the fastest and most student-friendly. All URLs below are live and verified as of 2026.

ABS Census QuickStats abs.gov.au/census → QuickStats → search Point Clare Population, housing, income, dwellings data by suburb. Searchable by suburb name. Free. ⭐ Best for Year 7. Easiest to navigate. Lots of graphable numbers in one place. Use the Dwellings or People section.
SEIFA Index (ABS) abs.gov.au → search SEIFA → 2021 Census Socioeconomic Advantage and Disadvantage score by suburb. A single score showing how advantaged or disadvantaged an area is. Good for comparing Point Clare to nearby suburbs (Gosford, Umina). Explains economic liveability.
BOM Climate Data bom.gov.au → Climate → Climate statistics → Gosford Monthly rainfall and temperature averages for Gosford (closest station to Point Clare). Historical data, free. Environmental factor. Students can graph average monthly rainfall across the year — 12 bars. Connects to liveability through climate comfort.
Central Coast Council Open Data data.centralcoast.nsw.gov.au Local infrastructure, parks, demographics, planning data for the Central Coast LGA. More complex to navigate. Better for stronger students or extension. Good for access-to-services data.
Fastest path to a complete Part B: Go to abs.gov.au/census → click QuickStats → type Point Clare → scroll to Dwellings section → find the dwelling structure table (separate house, semi-detached, flat/apartment, other). Those four numbers become your column graph. Takes under 3 minutes to find. That is the data. Now draw the graph.

Step 2 — Teach BOLTSS. Write it on the board.
BOLTSS — every graph needs all five

Border around the graph area
Orientation (usually vertical columns for this task)
Legend (if more than one data set)
Title (what the graph shows, including place name and year)
Scale (Y-axis numbered, units shown, e.g. number of dwellings or %)
Source (who collected the data — ABS 2021 Census)

Step 3 — Source validation. Three questions. Students write these down.
Source validation questions — Part B, Step 1

1. Who made this data? — Australian Bureau of Statistics (ABS), an independent Australian Government agency.

2. Is this a reliable source? Why? — Yes. The ABS is the official national statistics agency. The data is collected by law from every household across Australia every five years.

3. Is it recent (within 5 years)? — Yes. The most recent Census was 2021. The next will be 2026.

Part B — WAGOLL (Point Clare dwelling types, ABS 2021)

Graph title: Dwelling Structure in Point Clare, NSW — 2021 (ABS Census)

Axes: X-axis — Dwelling type (Separate house / Semi-detached / Flat or apartment / Other). Y-axis — Number of dwellings (0–1,200). Source line: Australian Bureau of Statistics, 2021 Census of Population and Housing.

Written interpretation (3–4 sentences):
The data shows that separate houses make up the overwhelming majority of dwellings in Point Clare, with semi-detached dwellings, flats and apartments making up a much smaller proportion. This suggests that liveability in Point Clare is shaped by a low-density residential environment, which provides space and privacy but may limit housing affordability and variety for different household types. One limitation of this data is that it only captures housing structure, not housing condition or affordability, meaning it cannot tell us whether residents can actually afford to live in the suburb. This is significant because housing cost is a major component of economic liveability, particularly for low-income households and renters.

Parts A + C
What to teach first — the definition
Definition — write on board, students copy

Environmental quality: the degree to which the natural and built environment of a place is clean, safe, healthy and pleasant to be in.

Physical influences vs human influences

Students need to classify features in their screenshots and photos using this framework.

Physical influences (natural)

Vegetation and tree cover
Water quality (Brisbane Water)
Landforms (slope toward water)
Natural hazards (flooding, fire risk)
Climate (rainfall, temperature)

Human influences (built)

Roads, traffic and noise
Industrial or commercial land use
Parks, gardens, open space
Waste management, litter
Building quality and design

Key point to make explicit: A river is a physical feature — but whether it is clean or polluted is a human decision. The Point Clare foreshore along Brisbane Water is a physical feature that human decisions have protected. That is a strong label for Part A.

2–3 sentence description — what students write at the end of Part A
WAGOLL — Part A description sentences

Point Clare's Google Earth screenshots reveal a predominantly residential suburb with direct access to the Brisbane Water foreshore, which is the most significant environmental quality feature of the area. The suburb is characterised by low-density housing, tree cover along residential streets and a protected waterway, all of which contribute positively to environmental quality. However, the limited commercial development within Point Clare means residents must travel to Gosford or East Gosford for many services, which affects economic liveability.

Part D + All parts
Part 1 — The paragraph model for Part D (15 min)

Part D is worth 20 marks. The paragraph model is the single most important thing for lifting grades in Part D. Teach it explicitly. Do not assume students know how to structure a geographical paragraph.

The four-part paragraph structure — write on board, leave it there

Point — state the liveability factor you are discussing
Evidence — describe specific evidence from your Parts A, B or C
Explanation — explain how this evidence affects liveability and for whom
Link — link back to your inquiry question

WAGOLL — full Part D example paragraph (Point Clare, social factor)
Point
One factor that affects liveability in Point Clare is access to social infrastructure, particularly recreational spaces along the Brisbane Water foreshore.
Evidence
As shown in my Part A Google Earth screenshot, Point Clare has a maintained foreshore reserve with picnic facilities and a sealed walking path directly adjacent to Brisbane Water. My Part C ground-level photographs confirm that the path is accessible and well-maintained.
Explanation
This improves liveability for elderly residents and young families by providing a free, local space for physical activity, social connection and relaxation. Access to quality green and blue spaces has been shown to reduce stress and improve mental health outcomes, which are key components of a liveable environment.
Link
Overall, this social infrastructure feature suggests that Point Clare has moderate-to-high liveability in terms of environmental and recreational access, although this benefit is not equally experienced by all residents — particularly those without a car who may find other parts of the suburb harder to access.
The recommendation sentence frame — students copy this into their booklet
Recommendation sentence frame

"To improve liveability in [Point Clare] for [specific group], I recommend [strategy] because [evidence from Parts A, B or C]. This would improve [factor] by [specific impact]."

WAGOLL — recommendation paragraph for Point Clare (elderly residents)

To improve liveability in Point Clare for elderly residents, I recommend installing additional shade structures and rest seating along the Brisbane Water foreshore walking path, because my Part C photographs show that the current path is largely exposed and lacks resting points. This would improve social and environmental liveability by making the path accessible to older residents during warmer months, reducing heat-related health risks and encouraging more frequent use of this valuable community space.

Part 2 — Planning strategies (10 min)

Students need one planning strategy for their recommendation. Four options — teach briefly, one example each.

Transport

Improve bus frequency on the 28 route linking Point Clare to Gosford station. Reduces car dependence. Improves liveability for non-drivers.

Green spaces

Add shade structures, seating and native planting to the foreshore reserve. Improves environmental and social liveability.

Accessibility

Upgrade kerb cuts and footpaths on Rawson Road. Improves liveability for wheelchair users and people with prams.

Zoning / land use

Zone a small local hub near the foreshore for a café, small supermarket or pharmacy. Reduces distance to essential services.

Part 1 — Peer edit checklist (20 min)

Students swap booklets. Partner checks against these criteria. Write one specific improvement suggestion per part — not "good job".

Part A checklist (Google Earth screenshots)

☐ At least 4 labels across up to 3 screenshots
☐ Each label names the feature
☐ Each label identifies the factor (environmental / social / economic / political)
☐ Each label explains the liveability connection — not just "it's good for people"
☐ Screenshot is from satellite view (Google Earth), not a street-level or photograph view
☐ 2–3 sentences describing liveability written below the screenshots

Part B checklist (column graph)

☐ Source validated — who made it, is it reliable, is it recent (within 5 years)
☐ URL recorded
☐ Graph has a title (including place name and year)
☐ Both axes labelled with units
☐ Source line on the graph (ABS 2021 Census etc.)
☐ 3–4 sentences: pattern described, liveability link explained, one limitation identified

Part C checklist (ground-level photographs)

☐ At least 4 labels across up to 3 photographs
☐ Photographs are ground-level (Google Street View, council site or news source) — not aerial
☐ Each label names the feature, factor and liveability effect
☐ 2–3 sentences include one clear positive and one clear negative observation with specific detail
☐ Source recorded for each photograph
