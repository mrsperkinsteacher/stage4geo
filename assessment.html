Vocabulary

Key terms for this assessment

These are the key geographical terms you need to know for this assessment. You must use at least six of them in your Part D written response.

Plurals and variations are accepted For example, amenities for amenity, or perceptions for perception.
Liveability
How suitable a place is to live in, based on factors such as access to services, safety, environmental quality, and social infrastructure.
Perception
The way a person sees or interprets a place, which may differ from another person's view depending on their age, background, or experience.
Environmental quality
The condition of the natural and built environment in a place, including air quality, green space, cleanliness, and the upkeep of infrastructure.
Amenity (amenities)
A feature of a place that makes it pleasant, convenient or comfortable to live in – such as parks, libraries, footpaths or recreational facilities.
Access to services
How easily people can reach essential services such as schools, hospitals, public transport, and shops.
Spatial distribution
The way something is spread across an area or region – for example, where services or green spaces are located.
Secondary data
Information that has already been collected by someone else, such as census data, statistics, or published reports.
Reliability
How trustworthy a source of information is – whether it is accurate, up to date, and from a credible organisation.
Interconnection
The way places, people, and environments are linked and affect each other.
Density
How concentrated something is within an area – for example, population density measures how many people live per square kilometre.
Planning strategy
A plan made by government or councils to manage how a place develops or improves over time.
Scale
The level at which something is studied or occurs – for example, local, regional, national or global scale.
Liveability index
A measure used to rank or compare how liveable different cities or places are, based on a range of factors.
Urban change
Changes that occur in towns and cities over time, such as growth, redevelopment, or changes in population.
Sustainability
Meeting the needs of people today without damaging the environment or reducing resources for future generations.
Wellbeing
A person's overall quality of life, including their physical health, mental health, safety, and social connections.
Community
A group of people who live in the same area or share common interests, values, or experiences.
Quantitative / Qualitative
Quantitative data uses numbers and statistics. Qualitative data uses descriptions, observations, or opinions.

A

Annotated Google Earth Screenshots

Up to 3 screenshots with at least 4 labels total

/10 marks
1

Open Google Earth and navigate to your place

Go to earth.google.com. Search for your chosen place. Try different zoom levels to show different aspects of the area.

2

Take up to THREE screenshots

On a MacBook: press Cmd + Shift + 4, drag to select the area, then drag the file from your Desktop into the table in your booklet.

You only need ONE screenshot, but up to three is fine if you want to show more.

🚨
More screenshots do not mean more marks. You only need one screenshot if it clearly shows four different features you can label. Extra screenshots are there to help you — not to earn you more marks. A student with one screenshot and four strong labels will score higher than a student with three screenshots and weak labels.
3

Write at least FOUR labels

Each label is one sentence that follows this formula:

The Label Formula
Feature name
+
[Factor]
+
how it improves liveability
"Gosford train station [Social/Economic] – provides access to transport which improves liveability by connecting residents to employment and services."
Common mistake – and how to fix it

Each label box needs ONE sentence, not a list of dot points. Here is the difference:

Not this

Car park [Social]

  • Helps people park safely
  • Has pedestrian crossing
  • Uses security cameras
  • Has disabled parking
This

"Car park [Social] – provides safe and accessible parking for shoppers, improving liveability by making services easy to reach."

Trick Take your best dot point idea and turn it into that one sentence structure. You do not have to use all four factors – if Social genuinely applies to two different features, that is fine. You need 4 labels total, each about something different.

The four factors are: Environmental, Social, Economic, Political. A label can use more than one if they genuinely both apply, e.g. [Social/Economic].

4

Write 2-3 sentences describing liveability

At the bottom of Part A, write a short paragraph describing what your screenshots show about liveability in your chosen place.

The screenshots of [place] show that liveability is [high/moderate/low] because…
One feature that improves liveability is… because…
Overall, the area appears [liveable/not very liveable] due to…
These sentences are not separately marked, but do not skip them – they are great practice for Part D and will help you feel confident when you get there.

Feature named specifically, correct factor, liveability link is clear and explains who benefits.

Precise geographical vocabulary used naturally, specific groups named, strong liveability reasoning throughout.

Label 1
Multi-use sports oval [Social] – the maintained oval supports both organised sport and informal recreation, improving liveability by building community wellbeing and giving young people in the area a safe space for physical activity.
Label 2
Accessible sealed car park [Social/Political] – the flat car park improves access to the park for elderly residents and people with disabilities, which increases liveability by making this community amenity available to everyone.

C Response

The data shows that income in Point Clare went up from 2011 to 2021.

This suggests that liveability in Point Clare is getting better because people have more money.

One limitation of this data is that it uses old information from 2021.

This is significant because things may have changed since then.

What is missing from your C response?
WhatC responseWhat a B response does instead
Pattern You said income went up. A B response uses the actual numbers from your graph.
Liveability link You said people have more money. A B response explains what more money actually lets people do, for example, afford housing or access services.
Limitation You named a limitation. A B response explains why that limitation is a problem for your conclusion.
Significance You said things may have changed. A B response says what specifically might have changed and why that matters.
What is missing from your B response?
WhatB responseWhat an A response does instead
Pattern You used the numbers. An A response looks at the numbers more carefully – is the increase getting bigger or smaller over time?
Liveability link You explained what more money lets people do. An A response asks whether that means liveability is good for everyone in the area, or just some people.
Limitation You explained why the limitation is a problem. An A response makes a judgement about how serious that limitation actually is.
Significance You gave a specific consequence. An A response ties it back to the question – what does this mean for how liveable Point Clare actually is?

Annotated Photographs

Up to 3 ground-level photographs with at least 4 labels total

/10 marks
Ground-level photographs only Do not use aerial or satellite images for Part C. Use Google Street View, a council website, or a reputable news source. You may visit the place in person if you would like to.
1

Find up to THREE ground-level photographs of your chosen place

Good places to look:

  • Google Street View – open the Find Your Place tab and click the Street View link for your place
  • Central Coast Council website – centralcoast.nsw.gov.au
  • A reputable local news source – such as the Central Coast Gosford Express Advocate

Record the source and date for each photograph in your booklet.

🚨
More photographs do not mean more marks. You only need one photograph if it clearly shows four different features you can label. Extra photographs are there to help you – not to earn you more marks.
2

Write at least FOUR labels using the same formula as Part A

The formula is the same as Part A:

The Label Formula
Feature name
+
[Factor]
+
how it affects liveability
"Cracked footpath [Environmental/Social] – damaged infrastructure reduces safety and accessibility for elderly residents and people with disabilities, reducing liveability."
Part C labels can be negative Unlike Part A where most features improve liveability, Part C photographs often show both positives and negatives. A label about something that reduces liveability is just as valid – and important for your evaluation in Step 3.
3

Write 2-3 sentences evaluating liveability

You must include one clear positive observation and one clear negative observation with specific detail. Use these sentence starters:

One positive aspect of liveability in [place] is… because…
However, a negative aspect is… which reduces liveability for…
Overall, the photographs suggest that liveability is [judgement] because…
These sentences are not separately marked, but do not skip them – they are great practice for Part D and will help you feel confident when you get there.
What does each grade look like? Check your assessment booklet rubric to see which grade you are aiming for
C Grade  |  5-6 marks

Labels 3+ features, liveability effect described for most, some evaluation present.

Label 1
Footpath [Environmental] – the footpath is broken which makes it hard for people to walk.
Label 2
Shops [Economic] – there are shops on the street which is good for the community.
B Grade  |  7-8 marks

Labels 4 features correctly, liveability effect clearly explained, who benefits mentioned, clear positive and negative evaluation.

Label 1
Cracked footpath [Environmental/Social] – the broken footpath is unsafe for elderly people and families with prams, which reduces liveability by making the area harder to get around.
Label 2
Row of shops [Economic] – the shops along the main street make it easy for local residents to buy what they need nearby, which improves liveability.
A Grade  |  9-10 marks

Labels 4+ features correctly, liveability effect explained precisely with specific detail, precise geographical vocabulary used naturally.

Label 1
Cracked footpath [Environmental/Social] – the uneven footpath reduces accessibility for elderly residents and people with disabilities, which lowers the liveability of the area because these groups cannot move through the space safely.
Label 2
Row of shops [Economic/Social] – the shops along the main street improve liveability by giving residents easy access to services and community amenity close to home, which means people do not have to travel far to meet their daily needs.

/20 marks
Before you write – use your notes from Parts A, B and C Your dot points, labels and graph interpretations are your evidence. You have already done the hard thinking. Part D is where you bring it all together.

Plan your response using the planner in your booklet

Your response needs four sections:

  • Introduction – state your place and overall liveability judgement
  • Factor Paragraph 1 – one liveability factor with evidence from A, B or C
  • Factor Paragraph 2 – a second liveability factor with evidence
  • Factor Paragraph 3 – a third liveability factor with evidence
  • Recommendation – one specific improvement, targeting a specific group of people
