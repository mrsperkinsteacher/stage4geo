Week 8 — Water Country — Aboriginal Water Management

Syllabus focus: Water in the World. Outcomes: GE4-APC-01, GE4-PER-01. Thread: Imitating Humility.

Cultural protocols for this week (please read first): use respectful, strengths-based, present-tense language when discussing Aboriginal Peoples' connections to water and Country. Capitalise Country, Aboriginal and First Nations. Do not retell specific cultural stories — use the published, permission-based Murray–Darling Basin Authority resource linked below, and point students to AIATSIS for further reading. If students design interview questions for a local Elder (Lesson 23), treat this as a genuine ethical-research exercise — questions are designed and discussed in class, not necessarily sent, unless your school has an established protocol for community engagement.
Source: PPT slides 55–57 · Worksheets 8.1, 8.2 (GTA NSW & ACT "Water in the World" Stage 4 Resource, 2026)
Week at a glance: L21 watches and responds to Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander perspectives on Water Country; L22 works through the Murray–Darling Basin's 'Caring for River Country' webquest; L23 designs ethical interview questions for a local Elder.
LESSON 21

Water Country Videos

Learning intention: we are learning how Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander knowledge systems contribute to sustainable water management today.

Lesson outline

  1. Starter (5 min): What do you think 'Country' means, beyond just 'land'?
  2. Teach (10–15 min): Introduce the concept of Water Country and Aboriginal Peoples' ongoing custodianship of it.
  3. Main task (20–25 min): Watch and respond to two videos on Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander perspectives on Water Country; identify how Indigenous knowledge systems contribute to sustainable water management today. (Worksheet 8.1)
  4. Exit ticket (5 min): Name one piece of knowledge from today's videos that is still used in water management today.

Main task instructions

  1. Watch both videos on Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander perspectives on Water Country.
  2. Note down examples of traditional knowledge mentioned in each.
  3. Answer: how does this knowledge contribute to sustainable water management today?

LESSON 22

Caring for River Country Webquest

Learning intention: we are learning why rivers hold deep significance for First Nations Peoples, and how this can inform water management today.

Lesson outline

  1. Starter (5 min): Read your school's Acknowledgement of Country — what does it say about land and waters?
  2. Teach (10–15 min): Introduce the Murray–Darling Basin Authority's "Caring for River Country" lesson package.
  3. Worldview (5 min): Micah 6:8 discussion (see box below).
  4. Main task (20–25 min): Work through the "Caring for River Country" case studies, then answer a three-tier differentiated inquiry question on why rivers matter to First Nations Peoples and how this can inform sustainable management today.
  5. Exit ticket (5 min): In your own words, why are rivers described as 'Country', not just 'a resource'?

Main task instructions

  1. Work through the Murray–Darling Basin Authority's "Caring for River Country" case studies.
  2. Take notes using the structured worksheet provided.
  3. Answer the inquiry question matching your tier (basic/middle/extension).

LESSON 23

Ethical Interview Design

Learning intention: we are learning the ethical principles for engaging with Aboriginal knowledge, and how to design respectful interview questions.

Lesson outline

  1. Starter (5 min): What makes a question 'respectful' when asking someone about their culture?
  2. Teach (10–15 min): Teach ethical research principles for engaging with Aboriginal knowledge and community members.
  3. Main task (20–25 min): Learn ethical principles for engaging with Aboriginal knowledge, then design respectful interview questions for speaking with a local Elder about caring for water Country. (Worksheet 8.2)
  4. Exit ticket (5 min): Why is it important to ask permission before recording or sharing someone's knowledge?

Main task instructions

  1. Read the ethical research principles provided.
  2. In pairs, draft three respectful interview questions about caring for water Country.
  3. Swap with another pair and check: are the questions respectful, open-ended and non-assumptive?
