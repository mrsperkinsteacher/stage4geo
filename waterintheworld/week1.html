Week 1 — Global Distribution of Water Resources

Syllabus focus: Water in the World. Outcomes: GE4-DFC-01, GE4-TAP-01. Thread: Caretaking Earth.

Source: PPT slides 4–11 (see iLearn) · Worksheets 1.1, 1.2
Week at a glance: L1 is a diagnostic on where the world's water actually is; L2 builds a sector graph and bar graph of global water types; L3 applies the PQE method to a world map of water access.
LESSON 1

Diagnostic: Where is the Water?

Learning intention: we are figuring out, as a class, where the world's fresh water actually is and who has access to it.

Lesson outline

  1. Starter (5 min): Brainstorm: where does your household's water actually come from before it reaches the tap?
  2. Teach (10–15 min): Introduce the unit and the idea that water, while renewable, is not evenly available.
  3. Worldview (5 min): Genesis 1:2 discussion (see box below).
  4. Main task (20–25 min): Diagnostic sort/brainstorm task: as a class, sort ideas about where freshwater is found and who has access to it.
  5. Exit ticket (5 min): One thing I already knew about water distribution, and one thing that surprised me.

Main task instructions

  1. As a class, brainstorm everywhere you think fresh water exists on Earth.
  2. Sort your ideas into 'easy to access' and 'hard to access'.
  3. Discuss: does more rainfall always mean more water per person? Why or why not?

LESSON 2

Sector and Bar Graphs of Global Water

Learning intention: we are learning to construct a sector graph and a bar graph to represent global water data.

Lesson outline

  1. Starter (5 min): Guess: what percentage of Earth's water is fresh, not salt?
  2. Teach (10–15 min): Explain the split between salt water, frozen fresh water, groundwater and accessible surface water.
  3. Main task (20–25 min): Construct a sector graph of the types of water on Earth, and a bar graph comparing freshwater availability across continents. (Worksheet 1.1)
  4. Exit ticket (5 min): Which continent has the least freshwater available, based on today's graph?

Main task instructions

  1. Using the data provided, calculate the size of each slice for your sector graph.
  2. Construct the sector graph accurately, labelling each type of water.
  3. Construct the bar graph comparing freshwater availability across continents, then answer the accompanying questions.

LESSON 3

Who Has Water? — PQE Map Analysis

Learning intention: we are learning to apply the PQE method (Pattern, Quantify, Exception) to a world map of water access.

Lesson outline

  1. Starter (5 min): Look at today's map for 10 seconds — what's the first pattern you notice?
  2. Teach (10–15 min): Model a PQE (Pattern–Quantify–Exception) description of a choropleth map of global water access.
  3. Main task (20–25 min): Independently write a PQE description of the choropleth map of global water access, explaining patterns via climate, development and location. (Worksheet 1.2)
  4. Exit ticket (5 min): Write one full PQE statement about today's map.

Main task instructions

  1. Study the worked PQE example on the map of global water access.
  2. Independently write your own Pattern, Quantify and Exception statements.
  3. Explain one exception on the map using climate, development or location.
