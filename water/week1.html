Week 1 — Water as a Resource

Syllabus focus: Water in the World. Outcomes: GE4-DFC-01, GE4-TAP-01. Thread: Caretaking Earth.

Week at a glance: L1 asks what makes something a resource at all; L2 looks at how little of the world's water is actually easy to use; L3 unpacks the seven factors that decide where water ends up.
LESSON 1

What is a Resource?

Learning intention: we are learning what makes something a renewable, non-renewable or continuous resource, and why water counts as renewable.

Lesson outline

  1. Starter (5 min): Mind-map: list every resource you can think of in 2 minutes.
  2. Teach (10–15 min): Define renewable, non-renewable and continuous resources with examples of each.
  3. Worldview (5 min): Genesis 1:2 discussion (see box below).
  4. Main task (20–25 min): Diagnostic concept task: sort a list of resources into the three categories, then justify why water is renewable even though the total amount of water on Earth never changes.
  5. Exit ticket (5 min): One sentence: 'Water is a ___ resource because ___.'

Main task instructions

  1. In pairs, sort the resource cards (or list) into renewable / non-renewable / continuous.
  2. Read the explanation of why water is classified as renewable — it cycles through evaporation, condensation and precipitation rather than running out.
  3. Write one paragraph explaining why water is a renewable resource, using the words 'finite' and 'water cycle'.

Christian worldview — Caretaking Earth

“The Spirit of God was hovering over the waters.”

Genesis 1:2

Water is present at the very beginning of creation — nothing in the natural world, including our lessons about it, starts from nowhere.

LESSON 2

Spatial Distribution of Global Water

Learning intention: we are learning how Earth's water is actually split up, and why so little of it is available for us to use.

Lesson outline

  1. Starter (5 min): Guess: what percentage of Earth's water do you think is fresh water we can actually drink?
  2. Teach (10–15 min): Teach the global split — about 97% salt water; of the remaining freshwater, most is locked up in ice caps/glaciers or held underground as groundwater; well under 1% is easily accessible surface fresh water.
  3. Main task (20–25 min): Construct a pie chart of global water distribution from the data provided, then peer-assess a partner's chart against a model.
  4. Exit ticket (5 min): Which is bigger: the amount of water frozen in ice, or the amount of easily accessible surface fresh water? By how much?

Main task instructions

  1. Using the data table provided, calculate the degrees for each slice of a pie chart (salt water, frozen fresh water, groundwater, accessible surface water).
  2. Construct the pie chart accurately with a ruler and protractor, labelling each slice with its percentage.
  3. Swap with a partner and check their chart against the model chart your teacher provides.

LESSON 3

Factors Influencing Water Availability

Learning intention: we are learning the seven factors that explain why some places have more fresh water available than others.

Lesson outline

  1. Starter (5 min): Recap: name one reason Australia might have more or less water available per person than a country with more rainfall.
  2. Teach (10–15 min): Explicit teaching of latitude, altitude, ocean currents, distance from the sea, geology, topography and climate change, using the modelled example of altitude and the Great Dividing Range (orographic rainfall).
  3. Main task (20–25 min): Modelled, then guided, then independent definition–description–example summaries for each factor.
  4. Exit ticket (5 min): In your own words, explain orographic rainfall in one sentence.

Main task instructions

  1. Copy the modelled summary for altitude (definition, description, example — the Great Dividing Range) and colour-code the three parts.
  2. Complete the guided class summary for topography together, using the same colour-coding.
  3. Independently write the summary for location and latitude, following the same structure, then share with a partner.
