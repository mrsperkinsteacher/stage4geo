Week 4 — Climate Graphs and the PQE Method — Skills Block

Syllabus focus: Water in the World. Outcomes: GE4-TAP-01, GE4-COM-01. Thread: Imagining Innovations.

Week at a glance: L10 teaches how to read a climate graph; L11 has students construct their own; L12 introduces the PQE method for describing maps and graphs.
LESSON 10

Reading Climate Graphs

Learning intention: we are learning to interpret a climate graph, including temperature, precipitation and hemisphere.

Lesson outline

  1. Starter (5 min): Look at the Grafton climate graph: which months look wettest?
  2. Teach (10–15 min): Teach that temperature is always a red line graph (left axis) and precipitation is always a blue bar graph (right axis); Southern Hemisphere summers fall in Dec/Jan/Feb, Northern Hemisphere summers in Jun/Jul/Aug.
  3. Main task (20–25 min): Answer question sets on climate graphs for several cities, identifying wettest/driest months, temperature range and hemisphere.
  4. Exit ticket (5 min): Is this climate graph from the Northern or Southern Hemisphere, and how do you know?

Main task instructions

  1. Study the worked example (Grafton) and note how temperature and precipitation are each read.
  2. Answer the question set for each city's climate graph provided.
  3. For each city, state whether it is in the Northern or Southern Hemisphere and justify your answer using the graph.

LESSON 11

Constructing a Climate Graph

Learning intention: we are learning to construct our own climate graph from a data table.

Lesson outline

  1. Starter (5 min): What two things does every climate graph need to show?
  2. Teach (10–15 min): Recap dual-axis construction: temperature as a line, precipitation as bars.
  3. Main task (20–25 min): Draw a climate graph from a provided data table, correctly plotting both axes.
  4. Exit ticket (5 min): Swap graphs with a partner and check one thing they did well.

Main task instructions

  1. Rule up dual axes: temperature (left) and precipitation (right).
  2. Plot the monthly temperature data as a connected line.
  3. Plot the monthly precipitation data as bars, then label your graph with a title, city and both axes.

LESSON 12

The PQE Method

Learning intention: we are learning to use the PQE method (Pattern, Quantify, Exceptions) to describe a map or graph.

Lesson outline

  1. Starter (5 min): Look at a choropleth map — what's the first pattern you notice?
  2. Teach (10–15 min): Teach Pattern (an obvious piece of information), Quantify (turn it into a number/percentage) and Exceptions (what doesn't fit the pattern), using a worked example on a water map.
  3. Worldview (5 min): Proverbs 25:2 discussion (see box below).
  4. Main task (20–25 min): Apply PQE independently to a water-stress choropleth map and a population/water cartogram.
  5. Exit ticket (5 min): Write one full PQE statement about today's map in the format P–Q–E.

Main task instructions

  1. Study the worked PQE example on the water map.
  2. Independently write a P, Q and E statement for the World Resources Institute's Water Stress by Country map.
  3. Try the same method on a cartogram (a map resized by data) — write a second PQE statement.
