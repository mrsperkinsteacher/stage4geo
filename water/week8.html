Week 8 — Water for our Food and Clothes — Fieldwork

Syllabus focus: Water in the World. Outcomes: GE4-MAN-01, GE4-TAP-01, GE4-COM-01. Thread: Seeking Justice.

Week at a glance: L22 looks at the water hidden in our food; L23 looks at the water hidden in our clothes; L24 is a fieldwork water audit, and the formal assessment task is due this week.
LESSON 22

Virtual Water in Food

Learning intention: we are learning about 'virtual' (embedded) water, and how much water goes into producing common foods.

Lesson outline

  1. Starter (5 min): Guess: how many litres of water does it take to make one glass of milk?
  2. Teach (10–15 min): Teach the concept of virtual/embedded water and compare the water footprint of different foods.
  3. Worldview (5 min): Matthew 25:35–40 discussion (see box below).
  4. Main task (20–25 min): Calculate and compare the litres of water used to produce a range of foods, and identify the most and least water-intensive.
  5. Exit ticket (5 min): Which surprised you more — the most or the least water-intensive food today? Why?

Main task instructions

  1. Use the GRACE Water Footprint Calculator or the provided data table to find the water footprint of several foods.
  2. Rank the foods from most to least water-intensive.
  3. Write two sentences on why you think some foods use so much more water than others.

Christian worldview — Seeking Justice

“I was thirsty and you gave me something to drink… whatever you did for one of the least of these, you did for me.”

Matthew 25:35–40

Jesus ties loving him directly to giving a thirsty person a drink; the water hidden in our food and clothes is a chance to notice needs we don’t normally see.

LESSON 23

Virtual Water in Clothing

Learning intention: we are learning about the water footprint of clothing, and fast fashion's impact on water use.

Lesson outline

  1. Starter (5 min): Guess: how many litres of water does it take to make one pair of jeans?
  2. Teach (10–15 min): Compare the water footprint of clothing items and discuss fast fashion's water impact.
  3. Main task (20–25 min): Compare the water footprint of several clothing items, then list personal strategies to reduce a clothing water footprint.
  4. Exit ticket (5 min): Name one realistic change you could make to reduce your own clothing water footprint.

Main task instructions

  1. Compare the water footprint data for several clothing items (e.g. jeans, a t-shirt, shoes).
  2. Discuss as a class: why does fast fashion use so much water?
  3. List three personal strategies to reduce a clothing water footprint.

LESSON 24

FIELDWORK: School/Home Water Audit — Assessment Due

Learning intention: we are learning to collect real fieldwork data on water use, and to calculate potential water savings.

Lesson outline

  1. Starter (5 min): Guess how many litres of water your household uses in a day.
  2. Teach (10–15 min): Explain the fieldwork water audit task and how to record and tally findings.
  3. Main task (20–25 min): Conduct a water audit at school or home (taps, toilets, irrigation); record, tally and calculate potential savings from fixing a leak or changing a habit. The Water Scarcity Case Study Task (10%) is due this week.
  4. Exit ticket (5 min): What is one water-saving change your household or school could make this term?

Main task instructions

  1. Using the fieldwork booklet, audit water use at school or home over the set period.
  2. Record and tally your findings in the provided table.
  3. Calculate the potential annual water saving from fixing one leak or changing one habit, and submit your Water Scarcity Case Study Task.
