Week 3 — Australia's Water Resources

Syllabus focus: Water in the World. Outcomes: GE4-DFC-01, GE4-TAP-01. Thread: Building Community.

Week at a glance: L7 explains why most Australians live where they do; L8 looks at regional Australia's reliance on groundwater; L9 compares Australia's rivers with the rest of the world.
LESSON 7

Australia, the Driest Inhabited Continent

Learning intention: we are learning why Australia's settlement patterns follow its water resources so closely.

Lesson outline

  1. Starter (5 min): Guess: what percentage of mainland Australia is semi-arid, arid or desert?
  2. Teach (10–15 min): Teach that around 70% of mainland Australia is semi-arid, arid or desert — the second driest continent on Earth, and the driest inhabited one.
  3. Worldview (5 min): Deuteronomy 8:7 discussion (see box below).
  4. Main task (20–25 min): Map and explain Australia's settlement patterns against its rainfall map, focusing on the east and south coasts.
  5. Exit ticket (5 min): In one sentence, why don't more Australians live in the centre of the country?

Main task instructions

  1. Compare a map of Australia's rainfall with a map of population density.
  2. Highlight the areas of highest population and describe their relationship to rainfall and coastline.
  3. Write a paragraph explaining why most Australians live where they do.

Christian worldview — Building Community

“The Lord your God is bringing you into a good land, a land with streams and pools of water.”

Deuteronomy 8:7

Israel was taught not to take a well-watered land for granted; Australians live on the driest inhabited continent and can fall into the opposite trap of assuming water will always be there.

LESSON 8

Groundwater and Regional Australia

Learning intention: we are learning how regional and inland towns rely on groundwater for their water security.

Lesson outline

  1. Starter (5 min): What do you think happens when a town's dam or river runs low?
  2. Teach (10–15 min): Explore groundwater as a source for inland and regional towns, including bores and the Great Artesian Basin.
  3. Main task (20–25 min): Case example of a regional Australian town whose water security depends on groundwater; students summarise the risks and benefits.
  4. Exit ticket (5 min): Name one risk of relying heavily on groundwater.

Main task instructions

  1. Read the case example of a groundwater-reliant regional town.
  2. List the benefits and risks of depending on groundwater as a main water source.
  3. Answer: what could this town do to reduce its risk if groundwater levels fall?

LESSON 9

Australia's Rivers vs the World

Learning intention: we are learning to construct and interpret a bar graph comparing Australia's water resources with the rest of the world.

Lesson outline

  1. Starter (5 min): Guess: where does the Murray-Darling Basin rank among the world's great river systems by length?
  2. Teach (10–15 min): Compare the Murray-Darling Basin with the Amazon, Nile, Congo, Mississippi, St Lawrence, Mekong and Danube.
  3. Main task (20–25 min): Construct a bar graph from the provided data and draw a conclusion about Australia's water resources relative to the rest of the world.
  4. Exit ticket (5 min): One sentence: is the Murray-Darling Basin large or small compared with the world's major rivers?

Main task instructions

  1. Using the river data table, construct a bar graph comparing the length of each river.
  2. Add a second bar graph (or dual axis) comparing average annual flow.
  3. Write a PQE (Pattern – Quantify – Exception) description of what the graphs show.
