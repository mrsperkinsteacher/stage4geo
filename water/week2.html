Week 2 — The Water Cycle and Catchments

Syllabus focus: Water in the World. Outcomes: GE4-PRI-01, GE4-TAP-01. Thread: Caretaking Earth.

Week at a glance: L4 revises the water cycle; L5 introduces catchments and drainage basins through the Murrumbidgee case study; L6 looks at how humans alter catchments, using the Snowy Hydro Scheme.
LESSON 4

The Water Cycle

Learning intention: we are learning the stages of the water cycle and how it connects places to each other.

Lesson outline

  1. Starter (5 min): Draw the water cycle from memory in 90 seconds — don't worry about being right yet.
  2. Teach (10–15 min): Teach evaporation, condensation, precipitation and collection, and how the cycle moves water between land, ocean and atmosphere regardless of borders.
  3. Main task (20–25 min): Label a water cycle diagram correctly and explain, in a sentence per stage, what is happening.
  4. Exit ticket (5 min): Which stage of the water cycle is happening right now, outside, today?

Main task instructions

  1. Label each stage of the provided water cycle diagram: evaporation, condensation, precipitation, collection.
  2. Under each label, write one sentence describing what is physically happening at that stage.
  3. Answer: how does the water cycle mean the same water keeps moving between countries, oceans and the sky?

LESSON 5

Catchments and Drainage Basins

Learning intention: we are learning what a catchment (drainage basin) is, and the key vocabulary geographers use to describe one.

Lesson outline

  1. Starter (5 min): Think-pair-share: what do you think 'catchment' means, based on the word itself?
  2. Teach (10–15 min): Teach watershed, tributary, confluence, source and mouth using the legacy drainage basin slides.
  3. Worldview (5 min): Psalm 24:1 discussion (see box below).
  4. Main task (20–25 min): Complete the cut-and-paste catchment cross-section diagram, then apply the vocabulary to the Murrumbidgee catchment (Yass Valley) case study — its area, major storages (Blowering, Burrinjuck and Talbingo Dams) and tributaries.
  5. Exit ticket (5 min): Name the five key catchment terms without looking at your notes.

Main task instructions

  1. Cut out, fold and glue the catchment cross-section diagram into your book, following the step-by-step instructions.
  2. Label the diagram with the five key terms: source, mouth, channel, confluence, tributary.
  3. Read the Murrumbidgee catchment case study and answer the questions on its size, major dams and tributaries.

LESSON 6

Managing Catchments

Learning intention: we are learning how humans have altered catchments for water supply and power, and how to weigh up the costs and benefits.

Lesson outline

  1. Starter (5 min): Quick recap quiz: define 'tributary' and 'watershed'.
  2. Teach (10–15 min): Case study: the Snowy Hydro Scheme as a large-scale human alteration of a catchment.
  3. Main task (20–25 min): Complete a SWOT analysis (strengths, weaknesses, opportunities, threats) of the Snowy Hydro Scheme.
  4. Exit ticket (5 min): Is the Snowy Hydro Scheme, on balance, a good example of managing a catchment well? Justify in one sentence.

Main task instructions

  1. Read the Snowy Hydro Scheme case study summary.
  2. In groups, complete a SWOT analysis of the scheme covering water supply, power generation and environmental impact.
  3. Individually, write a judgement paragraph on whether the scheme manages the catchment sustainably.
