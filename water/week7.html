Week 7 — The Value of Water

Syllabus focus: Water in the World. Outcomes: GE4-PER-01, GE4-APC-01. Thread: Embracing Diversity.

Cultural protocols for this week (please read first): use respectful, strengths-based, present-tense language when discussing Aboriginal Peoples' connections to water and Country. Capitalise Country, Aboriginal and First Nations. Do not retell specific cultural stories — point students to published, permission-based sources such as AIATSIS. Refer to "the Aboriginal Country your school stands on" rather than naming a specific Country unless your school's Acknowledgement of Country already does so.
Week at a glance: L19 introduces the four ways water can be valued; L20 explores First Nations custodianship of water and Country; L21 studies the spiritual and cultural significance of water in India.
LESSON 19

Economic, Cultural, Aesthetic and Spiritual Value

Learning intention: we are learning the four ways water can be valued, beyond simply being useful.

Lesson outline

  1. Starter (5 min): Besides drinking it, why else might water matter to a person or a community?
  2. Teach (10–15 min): Teach economic, cultural, spiritual and aesthetic value, with examples of each.
  3. Main task (20–25 min): Sort a set of real-world examples into the four value categories.
  4. Exit ticket (5 min): Give one example of water's aesthetic value that isn't in your notes.

Main task instructions

  1. Copy the four categories of value with a definition of each.
  2. Sort the example cards (or list) into the correct category.
  3. Write one original example of your own for each category.

LESSON 20

First Nations Custodianship of Water and Country

Learning intention: we are learning about Aboriginal Peoples' ongoing custodianship of water sources and Country.

Lesson outline

  1. Starter (5 min): Read your school's Acknowledgement of Country aloud together — what does it say about land and waters?
  2. Teach (10–15 min): Explore Aboriginal Peoples' ongoing (present-tense) custodianship of water and Country, pointing to AIATSIS rather than retelling specific stories.
  3. Main task (20–25 min): Reflect on the wording of the Acknowledgement of Country and its reference to waters; explore the AIATSIS website for published material on water custodianship.
  4. Exit ticket (5 min): In your own words, what does 'custodianship' of water mean?

Main task instructions

  1. Discuss as a class what the school's Acknowledgement of Country says about land and waters, and why.
  2. Browse the AIATSIS website (teacher-guided) for published material on Aboriginal Peoples' connections to water.
  3. Write a short reflection on what custodianship of water means, in your own words.

LESSON 21

Case Study: India's Sacred Rivers

Learning intention: we are learning about the spiritual, cultural and economic significance of the Ganges and Yamuna Rivers in India.

Lesson outline

  1. Starter (5 min): Can you think of a river, lake or body of water that means something special to you or your family?
  2. Teach (10–15 min): Case study: the Ganges and Yamuna Rivers — spiritual and cultural significance alongside economic and domestic use.
  3. Worldview (5 min): Acts 17:26 discussion (see box below).
  4. Main task (20–25 min): Complete a Venn diagram comparing the value of water in Australia and in India.
  5. Exit ticket (5 min): Name one way water is valued similarly in Australia and India, and one way it differs.

Main task instructions

  1. Read the case study on the Ganges and Yamuna Rivers.
  2. List the spiritual, cultural and economic uses/values described.
  3. Complete a Venn diagram comparing the value of water in Australia and in India.

Christian worldview — Embracing Diversity

“From one man he made all the nations… and he marked out their appointed times in history and the boundaries of their lands.”

Acts 17:26

Every culture’s particular relationship to its land and water is part of how God has ordered the nations — worth understanding on its own terms.
