Stage 4 Geography | Year 7 | Interconnections and Trade

Week 1 — What connects us?

Personal, recreational and cultural connections that link individuals to people, places and environments. Outcomes: GE4-PRI-01, GE4-COM-01. Thread: Building Community.

Unit essential question: How am I connected to people and places I will never see?
Lesson 1

What connects us? Defining interconnection

Learning intention

We are learning what geographers mean by interconnection, and beginning to explore how our own lives are connected to people and places near and far.

Lesson outline

  1. Starter (5–10 min): "Before 9am today, list everything that connected you to another place" — breakfast food origins, phone, clothes, the bus. Students list on the worksheet; share a few aloud.
  2. Diagnostic (10 min): Students complete 8 quick questions on the worksheet, done silently and individually. Reassure them: "This is not a test — it shows your teacher what you already know." Keep these to compare at the end of the unit.
  3. Teach (10 min): Build a class definition of interconnection — steer towards "the relationships between people, places and environments". Introduce the unit essential question and have students copy both onto the worksheet.
  4. Main task (20 min): Sketch map of personal connections (below).
  5. Worldview + exit ticket (5–10 min).

Main activity instructions

  • Model an example sketch map on the board first: home in the centre, lines out to a relative's town, a favourite food's country of origin, a sporting team's home ground.
  • Students draw their own on worksheet page 2: home at the centre; lines to places connected via family, food, media, sport or holidays; different line styles for different connection types.
  • Require a title, a key/legend and a north arrow — introduce BOLTSS informally (today students only use three parts: Title, Legend/key and Orientation/north arrow).

Christian worldview — Building Community

1 Corinthians 12:12–27 — one body, many parts. God designed creation for interdependence; our connections are part of the design, not an accident.

Discussion: "If we're all connected like parts of a body, what happens when one part is hurting?"

Lesson 2

Shared culture, shared places

Learning intention

We are learning how culture and recreation connect people to places, and how geographers use surveys to collect information about connections.

Lesson outline

  1. Starter (5–10 min): Photo prompt discussion — what places bring people together in your local area? (a beach, a sporting ground, weekend markets, places of worship). Students fill the Task 1 table.
  2. Teach (10 min): How culture and recreation link people to places — examples: surf lifesaving clubs, weekend sport, local markets, religious gatherings. Key idea: repeated shared activities turn a space into a place people feel connected to.
  3. Main task (25 min): Design and run a class connections survey (below).
  4. Data reading (5–10 min): Students practise reading a sample survey results table on the worksheet (labelled sample data for classroom use).
  5. Worldview + exit ticket (5 min).

Main activity instructions

  • Teach the three question types first: yes/no, multiple choice, and scale (1–5) — examples are on the slides and worksheet.
  • Each student drafts two questions on travel, technology and trade in students' lives (worksheet Task 3), labelling the type.
  • As a class, agree on a final survey of no more than 10 questions — collect suggestions on the board and vote or curate.
  • Every student then answers the final survey (paper hands-up tally on the board works fine). Keep the class results — they can be graphed in a later lesson.

Christian worldview — Building Community

Acts 2:44–47 — the early church shared meals, possessions and daily life; community grew out of shared places and shared time.

Discussion: "What do shared places teach us about how God designed community?"

Lesson 3

How connected is my life?

Learning intention

We are learning to identify and map the many ways our daily lives are connected to people and places around the world.

Lesson outline

  1. Starter (5 min): "Guess: how many countries were involved in getting your phone into your hand?" Students record a guess, discuss, then update it. (A phone's minerals, parts, design, assembly and shipping span many countries — far more than most students guess.)
  2. Main task 1 (10–15 min): App and technology audit — students list 6 apps/technologies in the worksheet table and record what each connects them to (people, goods, information, places).
  3. Main task 2 (20 min): World map task (below).
  4. Writing (10 min): Summary paragraph using the sentence scaffold.
  5. Worldview + exit (5 min): one-sentence answer to the essential question.

Main activity instructions

  • The worksheet includes a blank world map outline. Students plot their personal connections — where their food, clothes, media and family come from — using the colour-coded key: green = food, blue = clothes, orange = media and entertainment, purple = family and friends.
  • For each connection: a coloured dot on the country, a line back to Australia. Add a title (BOLTSS).
  • Summary paragraph scaffold: "My life is connected to ___ places across ___ continents. My strongest connections are… This shows that…"
  • Helpful resource for origins of everyday products: TED-Ed and Behind the News (BTN) have short clips on global trade — search: "BTN global supply chains" or "TED-Ed where does your smartphone come from".

Christian worldview — Building Community

1 Thessalonians 5:18 — "Give thanks in all circumstances." Behind every connection is a person: a farmer, a factory worker, a ship's crew, a designer. Gratitude turns an invisible supply chain into people worth thanking God for.

Discussion: "Who is one unseen person behind something you used today — and what would you thank them for?"
