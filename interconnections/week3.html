Stage 4 Geography | Year 7 | Interconnections and Trade

Week 3 — Travel and Transport Networks

How transport networks connect people to goods, services, places and people in different locations. Outcomes: GE4-PRI-01, GE4-TAP-01, GE4-PER-01. Thread: Seeking Justice.

Lesson 7 — How travel has changed

Learning intention: We are learning how transport technology has changed travel over time and made the world feel smaller.

Lesson outline

  1. Starter: compare flying in the 1950s (dressing up, fares worth months of wages, smoking on board) with budget flying today. Think–pair–share: what surprises you most?
  2. Teach: time-space convergence in student-friendly terms — the world feels smaller because travel is faster and cheaper. Case study: Sydney to London on the 1947 Qantas ‘Kangaroo Route’ (about 4 days, many stops) versus today (about 22 hours, one stop).
  3. Skill: model how to construct a line graph (title, even scales, labelled axes, plot, rule) using the worked example on the slides, then model the trend-description scaffold.
  4. Task: students construct a line graph of overseas departures on the worksheet grid and describe the trend.
  5. Exit ticket: three quick questions on the final slide.

Activity instructions

  • Worksheet Task 2 uses a data table labelled “Sample data based on Australian Bureau of Statistics overseas departures trends” (1980–2024, including the 2020 pandemic collapse). Students plot seven points on the pre-drawn grid with pencil and ruler.
  • Insist on real numbers in the trend description: “Between ___ and ___, departures rose/fell from ___ to ___…” The scaffold is on the slide and the worksheet.
  • Non-specialist tip: the only “exception” students need to spot is 2020, when border closures cut travel to almost nothing.

External links

  • Australian Bureau of Statistics — overseas arrivals and departures: abs.gov.au (search phrase: ABS overseas arrivals and departures)
  • Qantas history of the Kangaroo Route — search phrase: Qantas Kangaroo Route 1947 history
Christian worldview — gratitude and perspective (Psalm 8:3–4): travel is a privilege most of the world’s people never experience. Discussion: who gets to travel, and who never does — is that just the way things are, or something worth caring about?

Lesson 8 — Transport networks

Learning intention: We are learning that transport networks are made of nodes and links, and how to describe the patterns they form.

Lesson outline

  1. Starter: “How does a banana from Ecuador reach your lunchbox?” — trace the journey on the slide and sort places (nodes) from journeys (links).
  2. Teach: networks = nodes (ports, airports, stations) + links (routes); describing spatial patterns (dense/sparse, connected/isolated); Sydney (Kingsford Smith) Airport as Australia’s busiest international gateway and an example of a hub.
  3. Skill: model maximum, minimum, range and rank with the worked example slide before students touch the worksheet data.
  4. Task: airport data table — calculate maximum, minimum and range, rank the top 5, then answer the pattern questions.
  5. Exit ticket: define node and link; complete a pattern sentence.

Activity instructions

  • The worksheet table (10 destination cities with weekly flight numbers) is labelled “Sample data for classroom use” — the pattern (strong Asia–Pacific connections, weak South America/Africa connections) is realistic and is what students should notice.
  • Expected answers: maximum 90 (Auckland), minimum 5 (Johannesburg), range 85. Top 5: Auckland, Singapore, Denpasar, Los Angeles, Tokyo.
  • Push students to use pattern vocabulary: dense, sparse, connected, isolated, hub.

External links

  • Flight-tracking map for a live view of the global network: flightradar24.com (great 2-minute hook if you have a projector)
  • Sydney Airport route information — search phrase: Sydney Airport international destinations
Christian worldview — building community (1 Corinthians 12:21): networks exist because people need each other; no place can supply everything alone. Discussion: what does a transport network teach us about depending on other people — and being dependable ourselves?

Lesson 9 — Transport and inequality

Learning intention: We are learning how access to transport differs between places, and how to make a written judgement about whether this is fair.

Lesson outline

  1. Starter: read the two scenarios aloud from the slide — a student in Tokyo catching the Shinkansen versus a farming family three hours from the nearest sealed road. Discuss how opportunities differ.
  2. Teach: case comparison — Japan’s Shinkansen (opened 1964, about 320 km/h, connects most major cities) versus Australia’s vast distances and the Inland Rail freight project being built between Melbourne and Brisbane; who benefits from transport investment and who misses out.
  3. Skill: teach the three-level judgement ladder explicitly from the slide — Level 1 describe, Level 2 explain (because…), Level 3 judge (“Although…, … therefore transport access is / is not fair because…”).
  4. Task: complete the Japan vs Australia comparison table, then write the three-level judgement using the worksheet frame.
  5. Exit ticket: one fact, one reason, one Level 3 sentence.

Activity instructions

  • The worksheet fact file gives all the figures students need (Japan ~330 people/km², Australia ~3 people/km², Shinkansen 1964 and ~320 km/h, Melbourne–Brisbane ~1,700 km) — these are well-known approximate facts, not invented data.
  • There is no single “right” judgement: reward students who use evidence at Level 3, whichever way they decide.
  • Non-specialist tip: Inland Rail carries freight, not passengers — students often assume it is a bullet train. The extension task explores this.

External links

  • Inland Rail project overview: inlandrail.com.au
  • Shinkansen network map — search phrase: Japan Shinkansen network map
Christian worldview — seeking justice (Proverbs 31:8–9): “Speak up for those who cannot speak for themselves” — access to transport shapes access to opportunity. Discussion: whose voice is missing when transport decisions are made?
