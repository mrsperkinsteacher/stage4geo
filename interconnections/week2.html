Week 2 — First Nations Connections and Economies

Syllabus focus: Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander Peoples’ connections to local, national and international economies. Outcomes: GE4-PRI-01, GE4-PER-01, GE4-COM-01. Thread: Seeking Justice.

Cultural protocols for this week (please read first): use respectful, strengths-based, present-tense language — connection to Country and First Nations economies are ongoing and contemporary, not historical relics. Capitalise Country, Aboriginal, First Nations. Do not retell specific cultural stories; point students to authoritative sources instead (AIATSIS, Common Ground, the National Museum of Australia). Refer to “the Aboriginal Country your school stands on” rather than naming places in shared materials.
Week at a glance: L4 explores what Country means and why we acknowledge it; L5 uncovers trade networks that crossed the continent long before 1788 and the First Nations economies operating today; L6 examines First Nations art as a global export and the injustice of fake ‘Aboriginal-style’ products.
LESSON 4

On Country Connections

Learning intention: we are learning what Country means to Aboriginal Peoples and why we acknowledge Country.

Lesson outline

  1. Starter (5 min): display the words of a generic Acknowledgement of Country (slide 2). Ask: what does each phrase mean? Why do we say it?
  2. Teach (15 min): what Country means — identity, law, responsibility and economy, not just land; connection to Country as an ongoing relationship (slides 3–4); how to use the AIATSIS map respectfully (slide 5); decode the key phrases together (slide 6).
  3. Worldview (5 min): Micah 6:8 discussion (slide 7).
  4. Main task (20 min): worksheet L4 (slide 8).
  5. Exit ticket (5 min): slide 9.

Main task instructions

  1. Students annotate an Acknowledgement of Country, explaining the meaning of each key phrase.
  2. ‘More than land’: one sentence each connecting Country to identity, law, responsibility and economy.
  3. Research: using the AIATSIS Map of Indigenous Australia, students record the Aboriginal Country your school stands on, plus one fact about the Traditional Custodians from a reputable source (and cite the source). Remind students the map shows general locations, not exact boundaries.
  4. Worldview reflection (2–3 sentences).

External links

  • AIATSIS Map of Indigenous Australia — aiatsis.gov.au/explore/map-indigenous-australia
  • Common Ground (background on connection to Country) — commonground.org.au
  • AIATSIS (Welcome and Acknowledgement explainer) — aiatsis.gov.au, search “Welcome to Country and Acknowledgement of Country”

Christian worldview — Seeking Justice

“He has shown you, O mortal, what is good… to act justly and to love mercy and to walk humbly with your God.”

Micah 6:8

Justice begins with truth-telling and respect. Discuss: why does knowing whose Country we are on matter to acting justly?

LESSON 5

Seen and Unseen Economies

Learning intention: we are learning that First Nations trade networks operated across Australia long before 1788, and that First Nations economies continue today.

Lesson outline

  1. Starter (5 min): “What did trade in Australia look like 1,000 years ago?” — think-pair-share, then challenge the assumption that trade arrived in 1788 (slide 2).
  2. Teach (15 min): pre-1788 trade networks — ochre from Wilgie Mia, Kimberley pearl shell traded across the continent, greenstone axe heads (slide 3); songlines as knowledge highways (slide 4); contemporary First Nations economies — art centres, cultural tourism, native foods, First Nations-owned businesses and ranger work (slide 5); why some economies are less visible (slide 6).
  3. Worldview (5 min): Amos 5:24 discussion (slide 7).
  4. Main task (20 min): worksheet L5 — card sort and map task (slide 8). You will need scissors; printed outline maps of Australia are ideal (students can sketch an outline in the framed box if not available).
  5. Exit ticket (5 min): slide 9.

Main task instructions

  1. Card sort: students cut out the 10 cards printed on the worksheet and order them from “highly visible to most Australians” to “less visible”, record the order, and justify one “less visible” placement. There is no single correct order — assess the reasoning.
  2. Map task: students plot three historical trade routes (described in text on the worksheet) on a map of Australia: A — Kimberley pearl shell travelling south-east across the continent; B — Wilgie Mia ochre traded east and south; C — greenstone axes from near present-day Melbourne traded north into New South Wales. Arrows, labels and a key required.
  3. Writing: 3–4 sentences — “Trade did not begin in 1788” — with two examples, including one contemporary economy.

External links

  • National Museum of Australia — nma.gov.au, search “Aboriginal trade routes”
  • AIATSIS (songlines from authoritative sources) — aiatsis.gov.au, search “songlines”
  • For a student-friendly video, search ABC BTN for “First Nations trade routes”.

Christian worldview — Seeking Justice

“But let justice roll on like a river, righteousness like a never-failing stream!”

Amos 5:24

Economies that were disrupted by colonisation deserve recognition. Discuss: what would it look like for justice to “roll on like a river” for First Nations economies today?

LESSON 6

Art in the Global Economy

Learning intention: we are learning how First Nations art connects communities to the global economy — and who is affected when fake ‘Aboriginal-style’ art is sold.

Lesson outline

  1. Starter (5 min): two images described on slide 2 — an authentic art-centre painting versus a mass-produced ‘Aboriginal-style’ souvenir. “What’s the difference? Who benefits from each sale?”
  2. Teach (15 min): First Nations art as a significant cultural export connecting remote communities to global markets (slide 3); the fake and AI-generated ‘Aboriginal-style’ art problem — the Productivity Commission’s 2022 study found most ‘Aboriginal-style’ souvenirs are not made or licensed by First Nations people (slide 4); responses, including the Indigenous Art Code (slide 5); analysing impacts at local, national and global scales (slide 6).
  3. Worldview (5 min): Proverbs 11:1 discussion (slide 7).
  4. Main task (20 min): worksheet L6 (slide 8).
  5. Exit ticket (5 min): slide 9.

Main task instructions

  1. Scale-of-impact table: who is affected by fake art at local, national and global scales, and how?
  2. Perspectives paragraph (PEEL): students choose one perspective — a First Nations artist, a tourist who unknowingly bought a fake, or a government regulator — and write a PEEL paragraph using the scaffold and sentence starters on the worksheet.
  3. Worldview reflection: “honest scales” in the souvenir industry (2–3 sentences).

External links

  • Indigenous Art Code — indigenousartcode.org
  • Productivity Commission, Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander visual arts and crafts study report (2022) — search “Productivity Commission Aboriginal visual arts and crafts study report 2022” or browse pc.gov.au
  • For a student-friendly video, search ABC BTN for “fake Aboriginal art”.

Christian worldview — Seeking Justice

“The LORD detests dishonest scales, but accurate weights find favour with him.”

Proverbs 11:1

Fake art is dishonest trade: buyers pay for culture and connection that were never there, and the real artists receive nothing. Discuss: what would “honest scales” look like in the souvenir industry?
