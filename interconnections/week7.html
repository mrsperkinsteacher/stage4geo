Stage 4 Geography | Year 7 | Interconnections and Trade — Week 7

Week 7 — The Journey of a T-Shirt

The core case study week: one good — a cotton t-shirt — investigated across its whole supply chain, with its environmental, sociocultural and economic impacts. Outcomes: GE4-PRI-01, GE4-COM-01. Thread: Seeking Justice.

Why this week matters: the syllabus requires an investigation of ONE good across a supply chain. This is that investigation, and Lesson 21 contains the week’s Inquiry Checkpoint — the key piece of assessable work (a structured PEEL paragraph). Everything in L19 and L20 builds towards it.

Lesson 19 — The life cycle of a t-shirt

Learning intention: We are learning to trace one good — a cotton t-shirt — through every stage of its supply chain, from cotton farm to disposal.

Lesson outline

  1. Starter (5 min): everyone looks at the tag inside their collar (or imagines one): “Guess how many countries touched your shirt before you.” Most guess too low — “Made in…” only names the last stop.
  2. Watch (6 min): TED-Ed “The life cycle of a t-shirt” with four watch-for prompts on the slide: where is the cotton grown? spun? sewn? sold?
  3. Teach (10 min): the eight stages — cotton farm (USA/China/India) → spinning and weaving (often China/India) → dyeing → garment factory (Bangladesh/Vietnam) → shipping → retail (Australia) → use and washing → disposal (landfill or export of used clothing). Includes a worked example of a strong vs weak annotation.
  4. Main task (20 min): worksheet — sequence the jumbled stages, annotate each with a place and what happens there, map the journey on the world map frame, then answer “Which stage surprised you most and why?”
  5. Exit ticket: three quick questions on the final slide.

Teacher notes

  • Facts used are stated carefully on the slides: about 2 billion t-shirts sold globally each year (TED-Ed figure), and roughly 2,700 litres of water for one t-shirt described as a commonly cited estimate (WWF).
  • The worksheet’s map task uses a framed placeholder — hand out a printed world map or let students sketch; approximate positions are fine, the point is the journey crosses the globe.
  • SUPPORT gives the first and last stages plus sentence starters; EXTENSION adds a ninth stage — used clothing export.

External links

  • TED-Ed, “The life cycle of a t-shirt”: youtube.com/watch?v=BiSYoeqb_VY (if the link changes, search phrase: TED-Ed life cycle of a t-shirt)
Christian worldview — Seeking Justice (Psalm 139:13): “you knit me together” — the Bible describes God as a garment-maker. The hands that made your shirt are unknown to you but fully known to God. Discussion: does it change how you treat your clothes, knowing each item has a story of real people?

Lesson 20 — Who made my clothes?

Learning intention: We are learning about the people who make our clothes, what happened at Rana Plaza, and who holds responsibility for how garment workers are treated.

Lesson outline

  1. Starter (5 min): play “The 2 Euro T-Shirt — A Social Experiment” (about 2 min): a vending machine in Berlin offered €2 t-shirts, then showed buyers who made them and offered a choice — buy or donate. Nine in ten chose to donate. Ask: the shirt didn’t change — what did?
  2. Teach (15 min): Rana Plaza (24 April 2013, Dhaka, Bangladesh — building collapse, over 1,100 garment workers killed; workers had reported cracks the day before); why fast fashion can be so cheap (low wages, long hours, weak safety rules, brand pressure for speed); the response — Fashion Revolution and “Who made my clothes?”, pushing brands towards transparency.
  3. Worked example: the opinion continuum, modelled with a justification that makes a claim and considers the other side.
  4. Main task (20 min): worksheet — opinion continuum (brands vs consumers, justified); sociocultural impacts table (workers, families, communities); three respectful questions students would ask a garment worker, then a reflection on what fair treatment would look like.
  5. Exit ticket: three quick questions on the final slide.

Teacher notes

  • Sensitive content: Rana Plaza involves the deaths of over 1,100 people. The materials keep it factual and dignified — no graphic images, no invented first-person testimony. The worksheet prints the dignity rule: we ask questions; we never invent workers’ words.
  • If any student has a family connection to Bangladesh or the garment industry, their knowledge is an asset — invite it rather than spotlighting it.
  • There is no teacher-approved “right” position on the continuum — mark the reasoning, not the location of the cross.

External links

  • “The 2 Euro T-Shirt — A Social Experiment”: youtube.com/watch?v=KfANs2y_frk (search phrase: 2 Euro T-Shirt social experiment)
  • Fashion Revolution: fashionrevolution.org (search phrase: Fashion Revolution who made my clothes)
Christian worldview — Seeking Justice (Luke 10:25–37): “And who is my neighbour?” The person who made my clothes is my neighbour — neighbour-love crosses distance. Discussion: the Samaritan crossed the road; what is the equivalent for us as consumers, and what might it cost?

Lesson 21 — Counting the cost (Inquiry Checkpoint)

Learning intention: We are learning to categorise the impacts of the t-shirt trade as environmental, sociocultural or economic, and to explain in a structured paragraph how one good connects people and places across the world.

Lesson outline

  1. Starter (5 min): quick sort — six impact statements sorted as environmental, sociocultural or economic (answers in the speaker notes).
  2. Teach (15 min): the three impact categories with t-shirt examples — environmental (water for cotton, dye pollution of rivers, textile waste in landfill, shipping emissions), sociocultural (working conditions, child labour risk, changed local cultures, jobs and skills), economic (jobs and export income for Bangladesh — garments make up most of its exports — cheap clothes for consumers, brand profits). Key idea: impacts can be positive AND negative at once — the garment industry has lifted many people, especially women, into paid work.
  3. Worked example: the PEEL paragraph built sentence by sentence on the slide, with the model opening sentence students may use word for word.
  4. Main task (25 min, protect 15 for the paragraph): worksheet — full impacts table (three categories × positive/negative), then the Inquiry Checkpoint paragraph.
  5. Exit ticket: three quick questions on the final slide.
★ Inquiry Checkpoint (the week’s key assessable work): a structured paragraph answering “Explain how a t-shirt connects people and places across the world through its supply chain.” Requirements: PEEL structure; integrates ONE geographical tool (the L19 supply chain diagram or a graph) and ONE concept (interconnection, scale or globalisation), both named. The worksheet provides the PEEL scaffold, a word bank and a model opening sentence. SUPPORT: a cloze version of the paragraph. EXTENSION: an added judgement — “Overall, does the t-shirt trade do more good or harm? Defend your answer.” Collect and mark this piece.

Teacher notes

  • Students need their L19 supply chain diagrams on their desks — the paragraph must refer to a geographical tool, and the diagram is the easiest choice.
  • Plan time backwards from the checkpoint; the paragraph needs at least 15 uninterrupted minutes.
  • The Bangladesh export fact is stated as “garments make up most of Bangladesh’s exports” — no invented percentages.
Christian worldview — Seeking Justice (Micah 6:8): justice, mercy and humility as the lens for judging trade — with a recap of the honest scales from earlier in the unit. Weighing the good and the harm honestly is itself an act of justice. Discussion: when you weigh up the t-shirt trade, what would “acting justly” ask of you this week?
