Stage 4 Geography | Year 7 | Interconnections and Trade — Week 9

Week 9 — Perspectives and Judgement, and the Topic Test

The unit’s summit: five stakeholders argue over the t-shirt, students write their final judgement on how fast fashion should be managed, and the week closes with the topic test. Outcomes: GE4-PER-01, GE4-MAN-01, GE4-COM-01. Thread: Seeking Justice.

Lesson 25 — Walking in their shoes: stakeholder roleplay

Learning intention: We are learning how different groups have different perspectives on how fast fashion should be managed.

Lesson outline

  1. Starter: “Same t-shirt, five very different views — how?” The slide shows the same $7 t-shirt circled by five voices. Draw out: a person’s situation shapes what they notice and want.
  2. Teach: perspective vs fact — a fact can be checked and is true for everyone; a perspective is a way of seeing shaped by situation, values and experience. Today’s rule: speak from your character’s perspective, supported by facts from the card.
  3. Roleplay: groups of five, one role card each — garment worker, fashion company executive, trade minister, NGO campaigner, teenage consumer. Timing: 5 min silent reading, 5 min preparation grid, 15 min structured discussion, 10 min debrief.
  4. Debrief: out of role — worksheet Task 3 (hardest position; which two stakeholders most need to cooperate; what surprised you), then class discussion.
  5. Exit ticket: “One thing I understand now that I didn’t before…” plus two quick checks.

Activity instructions

  • Cut up one set of the five role cards per group of five before the lesson. Groups of four: drop the trade minister. Groups of six: two consumers is realistic.
  • Place confident speakers in the harder roles (executive, trade minister); the consumer role suits anxious students — it is the most familiar.
  • In the discussion, each stakeholder speaks 1 minute uninterrupted first, then open discussion. Group goal: one change to how fast fashion is managed that all five could accept. Not reaching agreement is a finding, not a failure.
  • Discussion rules (on the slide, leave it up): speak in role; disagree with ideas, not people; no one speaks twice until all five have spoken once.
  • Framing matters: the garment worker is skilled and proud, not a victim — she values the job and wants it made safe and fair, and fears brands leaving. Keep that dignity in the room.
  • Non-specialist tip: if a group stalls, feed in a spark — “Minister, the campaigner just said your laws are too weak. Respond.”

External links

  • Background on garment industry working conditions — search phrase: Bangladesh garment industry working conditions BBC
  • How brands report on supply chains — search phrase: fashion transparency index
Christian worldview — Seeking Justice (Philippians 2:3–4): “In humility value others above yourselves… each of you to the interests of the others.” Empathy as a discipline — students literally spent the lesson looking to the interests of others. Discussion: why is it hard to truly listen to a view you disagree with? What does humility offer here?

Lesson 26 — Making a judgement: which strategy should come first?

Learning intention: We are learning to make and justify a judgement about how fast fashion should be managed.

Lesson outline

  1. Starter: three sample answers to “Should fast fashion be managed more strictly?” are on the slide — one only describes, one explains, one judges. Vote: which is strongest, and why?
  2. Teach: the describe → explain → judge ladder, revealed against the three models, with signal words for each rung (describe: is / has / shows; explain: because / so / this leads to; judge: should / most important / although).
  3. The question: “Which management strategy should be prioritised for fast fashion, and why?” Recap the four options from Week 8 — consumer choices, company codes of conduct, government laws, certification — with one strength and one limit each.
  4. Task: worksheet — planning grid first (about 8 min), then one full paragraph (8–12 sentences) in the writing frame with the checklist in the margin.
  5. Exit ticket: name the ladder’s three rungs; state one limit of your chosen strategy — plus the reminder that the topic test is next lesson.

Activity instructions

  • Success checklist (also in the worksheet margin): names ONE strategy; TWO reasons each backed by evidence from the unit; considers ONE alternative; uses at least THREE geographical terms.
  • Any of the four strategies can earn full credit — assess the reasons, evidence and weighing, not the choice.
  • Insist the plan is finished before writing starts; a complete planning grid makes the paragraph almost write itself.
  • Support: the cloze paragraph replaces Task 2 and still hits every checklist item. Extension: a second paragraph from the perspective of a stakeholder who would disagree — yesterday’s role cards are the menu.
  • Collect the paragraphs — this is the best single piece of evidence of unit learning.
  • The three starter answers are sample texts written for classroom use.

External links

  • Certification schemes students may cite — search phrase: Fairtrade cotton certification how it works
  • Government action on supply chains — search phrase: Australia Modern Slavery Act supply chains
Christian worldview — Seeking Justice (Micah 6:8): the unit’s compass verse returns — act justly (choose strategies that protect the vulnerable), love mercy (grace for imperfect efforts, including our own shopping), walk humbly (our knowledge is partial). Discussion: what would “walking humbly” look like when arguing for your strategy?

Test day — topic test

Purpose: students sit your topic test on Interconnections and Trade under calm, quiet conditions. The slides do the housekeeping so you can do the reassuring.

Running the lesson

  1. Entry: title slide up as students arrive — “You are ready for this.” Calm teacher, calm room.
  2. Before we start: equipment, silence, read every option, about 1 minute per question.
  3. Warm-up: six key terms flashed on screen; students recall each meaning silently. Confidence-building, not testing.
  4. During the test: leave the “Test in progress” slide on screen. Five-minute and one-minute warnings, delivered quietly.
  5. Early finishers: re-check every answer, then on scrap paper: “Which question made you think hardest, and why?”
  6. After papers are collected: the gratitude close (1 Thessalonians 5:18) — “whatever the result, your worth isn’t a mark” — and a genuine thank-you for the term’s effort.
Revision checklist — share with students before test day.

Twelve key terms I can define:

interconnectiontradeimportexportsupply chaintrade flowglobalisationtariffsustainabilitystakeholderperspectivecertification

Five skills I can do:

  1. Trace a product’s supply chain from raw material to consumer, naming the steps.
  2. Read a map of trade flows and describe the pattern using direction and place names.
  3. Interpret a line graph, column graph or pie chart about trade, and pick out the main trend.
  4. Explain a cause → effect → outcome chain (for example, how one influence or shock changes a trade flow).
  5. Distinguish describing, explaining and judging — and recognise a management strategy and a stakeholder’s perspective.

Best revision: this checklist plus the student’s own completed worksheets from the unit.

Notes for the teacher

  • The test itself is the existing multiple-choice topic test for this unit — nothing on the slides names questions or content from it.
  • No result talk on the day, no “that was easy, right?” afterwards — students who struggled are listening.
  • Collect papers row by row and count them before showing the final slide.
Christian worldview — a grateful close (1 Thessalonians 5:18): “Give thanks in all circumstances.” Shown only after papers are collected: whatever the result, a student’s worth isn’t a mark — and a term of honest effort is worth being thankful for.
