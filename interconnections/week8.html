Stage 4 Geography | Year 7 | Interconnections and Trade — Week 8

Week 8 — Managing Sustainability: Ethical Fashion and Palm Oil

Strategies to manage sustainability in the production and consumption of goods — the t-shirt as the core case, palm oil as the supporting study. Outcomes: GE4-MAN-01, GE4-TAP-01. Thread: Caretaking Earth.

Lesson 22 — Ethical fashion strategies

Learning intention: We are learning strategies that consumers, companies and governments can use to make the t-shirt trade more sustainable and fair.

Lesson outline

  1. Starter: “You need a new t-shirt. List every option you have that ISN’T buying a brand-new cheap one.” Every answer students give (op shop, mend, swap, buy quality that lasts, buy certified…) is already a management strategy — tell them so.
  2. Teach: management strategies at three levels — CONSUMERS (buy less, buy second-hand, check the label, use the Baptist World Aid Ethical Fashion Guide), COMPANIES (ethical sourcing codes, factory audits, transparency reports, Fairtrade cotton), GOVERNMENTS (modern slavery laws, import standards, the binding factory safety accord created after the Rana Plaza collapse in Bangladesh).
  3. Certification: what a real certification logo promises — a standard checked by an outside organisation, not a company’s own green marketing.
  4. Worked example: classify one strategy card together (who acts?), then model a ranking justification before releasing the task.
  5. Task: worksheet — classify 8 strategy cards by level, rank the top 3 with justification, personal pledge box.
  6. Exit ticket: three quick questions on the final slide.

Activity instructions

  • Task 1 answers: consumer — cards 1, 4, 7; company — cards 2, 5, 8; government — cards 3, 6. Card 6 (the safety agreement) is government-brokered but signed by companies — accept either classification if the reasoning is sound.
  • Task 2 has no single right answer — assess whether justifications use lesson vocabulary (audit, transparency, certification, compulsory vs voluntary).
  • Task 3 pledges must be realistic for a 12-year-old: mending, op-shopping, clothes swaps, asking parents to check the Ethical Fashion Guide.
  • Non-specialist tip: the key contrast between the levels is that consumer and company strategies are voluntary, while government laws are compulsory — that distinction powers most good rankings.

External links

  • Baptist World Aid Ethical Fashion Guide — baptistworldaid.org.au
  • Fairtrade in Australia — fairtrade.com.au
  • The Bangladesh safety accord — search phrase: Bangladesh Accord factory safety Rana Plaza
Christian worldview — Seeking Justice & Caretaking Earth (Isaiah 58:6–7): “Is not this the kind of fasting I have chosen: to loose the chains of injustice…” — worship includes how we treat the people our shopping connects us to. Discussion: can shopping be an act of worship or justice? How?

Lesson 23 — The palm oil problem

Learning intention: We are learning what palm oil is, where it is produced, and why managing its impacts on people and environments is complicated.

Lesson outline

  1. Starter: product hunt on the slide — chocolate spread, instant noodles, shampoo, biscuits, lipstick, ice cream. “What do these share?” Reveal: palm oil, found in about half of packaged supermarket products (WWF figure).
  2. Teach: what palm oil is and why manufacturers love it — cheap, versatile, and the highest-yielding oil crop per hectare (which is also why simply switching crops could clear more forest elsewhere).
  3. Where it’s grown: Indonesia and Malaysia produce about 85% of the world’s palm oil; the crop’s tropical climate needs overlap exactly with rainforest zones — that overlap is the heart of the problem.
  4. The tension: deforestation, orangutan/tiger/elephant habitat loss in Borneo and Sumatra, peatland carbon emissions — AND millions of livelihoods including smallholder farmers. Both columns are true at once; the tension is the point.
  5. Worked example: the dilemma chain — ban → switch crops → more land needed → forests cleared elsewhere; plus the livelihoods cost.
  6. Task: worksheet — pie chart construction (degrees = % × 3.6), take-home label hunt using 12 alternative ingredient names, and the dilemma writing task.
  7. Exit ticket: three quick questions on the final slide.

Activity instructions

  • Task 1 answers: Indonesia 212.4°, Malaysia 90°, all others 57.6° (accept 212 / 90 / 58 with rounding — angles must total 360°). The producer table is labelled sample data for classroom use; the verifiable headline is that Indonesia and Malaysia together produce about 85%.
  • Task 2 is homework-friendly — set a due date. Remind students that some names (stearic acid, sodium lauryl sulfate, glyceryl stearate) can also be made from other oils, so they are clues rather than proof: “maybe” is a legitimate answer.
  • Task 3 strongest answers give the land-swap reason AND the livelihoods reason. The model answer is on the worked-example slide.
  • Non-specialist tip: if students conclude “palm oil is evil, ban it”, the lesson hasn’t landed — the high-yield point means a ban could shift deforestation to other crops and countries while stripping income from smallholder farmers.

External links

  • WWF Australia on palm oil — wwf.org.au (search the site for palm oil)
  • Orangutan habitat and deforestation — search phrase: WWF orangutan palm oil Borneo Sumatra

Lesson 24 — Strategies that work? Evaluating palm oil management

Learning intention: We are learning to evaluate strategies for managing the sustainability of palm oil production and consumption, including RSPO certification.

Lesson outline

  1. Starter: recap the dilemma from L23, then the cliff-hanger: “If banning isn’t the answer, what is?” Almost every student suggestion turns out to be a version of certification or scorecards.
  2. Teach: RSPO certification — no clearing of primary forest, fair treatment of workers and communities, traceability through the supply chain. “The palm oil version of Fairtrade cotton.”
  3. Spotting it: the certification logo on packs, WWF company scorecards, and barcode-scanning apps.
  4. Weighing: evidence for (growing certified share, standards, market reward) and against (critics say standards are too weak, enforcement is hard, much palm oil is still uncertified). Evaluating = weighing both sides, then judging.
  5. Strategy mapping: strategies operate at the production end (Indonesia/Malaysia) AND the consumption end (Australia/Europe) — interconnection applied one last time.
  6. Worked example: describe–explain–judge modelled on a boycott, so students must transfer the structure (not the content) to certification.
  7. Task: worksheet — evaluation table (RSPO vs boycott vs scorecards), data check on the certified share across a decade, final judgement mini-paragraph.
  8. Exit ticket: three quick questions on the final slide.

Activity instructions

  • Task 1: strengths and weaknesses can be lifted from the weighing slide, but insist on full sentences. A useful weakness for boycotts: they punish responsible growers and smallholders along with bad actors.
  • Task 2 uses sample data for classroom use (certified share rising from 4% to 20% across a decade). Question (b) is the subtle one — the data shows certification growing, not that deforestation fell; a top answer spots that it measures uptake, not outcomes.
  • Task 3 is the assessable piece: mark for describe–explain–judge structure and a defended verdict. Positive, negative and mixed verdicts are all acceptable if justified.
  • Non-specialist tip: “evaluate” in Geography means weighing evidence on both sides before judging — a paragraph with no weakness, or a verdict with no “because”, hasn’t evaluated.
