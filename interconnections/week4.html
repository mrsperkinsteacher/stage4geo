Week 4 — Technology Connects

Technologies that connect people to goods, services, places, information and people in different locations. Outcomes: GE4-PRI-01, GE4-TAP-01, GE4-COM-01. Thread: Building Community.

Lesson 10 — A connected world

Learning intention: We are learning how technology connects people to goods, services, information, places and other people in different locations.

Lesson outline

  1. Starter: “In 1990, to hear a song you had to buy the album or wait for the radio” — pairs list everything a smartphone has replaced (camera, street directory, letters, encyclopaedia, Walkman, bank branch…).
  2. Teach: define globalisation — the increasing movement of goods, services, money, people and ideas around the world — then show how technology drives it: undersea cables carry about 99% of international data, container tracking moves goods reliably, online shopping connects buyers to sellers worldwide.
  3. History beats: a fast timeline — telegraph → telephone → internet → smartphone — each invention made distant places feel closer.
  4. Worked example: model analysing one technology (video calls): what it connects people to, one benefit, one risk.
  5. Task: worksheet table of six technologies, then the mini paragraph “The technology that has changed my family's connections most is… because…”.
  6. Exit ticket: three quick questions on the final slide.

Activity instructions

  • Worksheet Task 2 covers six technologies: undersea cables, smartphones, GPS, online marketplaces, video calls, contactless payment. For each: what it connects people to (goods / services / information / places / people), one benefit, one risk.
  • Push back on lazy answers: “it's faster” is not a benefit until it says faster at what, for whom; “it can break” is not a risk — steer towards privacy, exclusion, scams.
  • Non-specialist tip: the one fact worth knowing cold is that about 99% of international data travels through fibre-optic cables on the ocean floor, not satellites.

External links

  • Interactive map of the world's undersea internet cables: submarinecablemap.com (a brilliant 2-minute hook — find the cables serving Australia)
  • Background on how global data travels — search phrase: how undersea cables carry the internet
Christian worldview — scatter or gather (Genesis 11; Acts 2): at Babel people were scattered; at Pentecost people from every nation were gathered and understood one another. Technology can do both. Discussion: does technology bring people together or push them apart — and when have you seen each?

Lesson 11 — Sector graphs

Learning intention: We are learning to construct an accurate sector graph using percentages, degrees and a protractor.

Lesson outline

  1. Starter: quick quiz — what fraction of a circle is 50%? 25%? 10%? — linking fractions to degrees and landing the key fact: 1% = 3.6°.
  2. Teach: what a sector graph shows (parts of a whole; the full circle = 100% = 360°) and when not to use one (change over time needs a line graph).
  3. Skill: the method — percentage × 3.6 = degrees — with a full worked example (class travel to school: car 50% = 180°, bus 25% = 90°, walk 15% = 54°, bike 10% = 36°) and explicit protractor steps.
  4. Task: students complete the degrees column for the internet-access data, construct the graph in the printed circle, and answer three interpretation questions.
  5. Exit ticket: convert 30% to degrees; state the two checks; when to use a sector graph.

Activity instructions

  • Equipment needed: protractors, rulers, sharp pencils, coloured pencils. The worksheet provides a large printed circle with the centre point and 12 o'clock starting line already marked.
  • The worksheet table “How Australians access the internet at home” is labelled “Sample data for classroom use”: mobile 45%, NBN/fixed 40%, satellite 5%, no access 10%. Expected degrees: 162°, 144°, 18°, 36° (total 360°).
  • Checkpoint: sight every student's degrees column before they draw — a wrong column makes the whole graph wrong.
  • The two classic protractor errors: measuring every angle from the 12 o'clock line instead of the previous sector's line, and reading the wrong scale (inner vs outer numbers).

External links

  • Australian Bureau of Statistics — internet and technology use: abs.gov.au (search phrase: ABS household internet access)
  • Protractor refresher for yourself if needed — search phrase: how to use a protractor to draw angles
Christian worldview — order and truthfulness (1 Corinthians 14:40): God is a God of order, and careful graphs help us see patterns in creation truthfully. An accurate graph is a quiet form of honesty; a distorted one misleads people with numbers. Discussion: why does it matter that our graphs tell the truth, even when no one would notice a small distortion?

Lesson 12 — The digital divide

Learning intention: We are learning what the digital divide is, who it affects, and why fair access to technology matters.

Lesson outline

  1. Starter: three scenario cards read aloud — the same homework task for a student with fast fibre in a city apartment, a student sharing phone data in a regional town, and a student with no home internet who relies on a library. Is this a fair race?
  2. Teach: define the digital divide; unpack “access” as four parts (device, connection, skills, affordability); who is most affected (remote communities, low-income households, older people, some First Nations communities); why it matters (school, work, banking, telehealth, staying connected).
  3. Global scale: about one-third of the world's people still do not use the internet (International Telecommunication Union estimate) — and the divide also exists inside wealthy countries like Australia.
  4. Worked example: model one well-built paragraph using Point → explain → result.
  5. Task: structured literacy response with sentence starters — describe the divide from the data table, explain two causes, explain two effects on young people, propose one realistic strategy.
  6. Exit ticket: define, name two affected groups, finish a strategy sentence.

Activity instructions

  • Sensitivity note: some students in the room may themselves have limited access. Keep every scenario in the third person and never ask students to disclose their own home internet situation.
  • The worksheet data table (internet access by location type) is labelled “Sample data for classroom use” — the pattern (access falls with remoteness) is the realistic point students should describe.
  • “Realistic” strategies must name who delivers them: libraries, councils, governments, telcos, charities, schools. The SUPPORT box lists workable examples.
  • Insist on the “this means…” sentence in every explanation — it is the sentence that turns a statement into an explanation.

External links

  • International Telecommunication Union — global connectivity facts and figures: itu.int (search phrase: ITU facts and figures internet use)
  • Australian digital inclusion research — search phrase: Australian Digital Inclusion Index
Christian worldview — wonderfully made (Psalm 139:14): every person is fearfully and wonderfully made, so nobody's worth depends on their connectivity — but access shapes opportunity, and fairness demands we care about who is left out. Discussion: whose voices don't get heard online?
