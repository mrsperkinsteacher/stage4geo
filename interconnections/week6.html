Stage 4 Geography | Year 7 | Interconnections and Trade — Week 6

Week 6 — What Shapes Trade? Trade Shocks and Data Skills

Economic, political and environmental influences that shape trade flows — and what happens when trade is shocked. Outcomes: GE4-PRI-01, GE4-TAP-01. Threads: Building Community, Caretaking Earth.

Lesson 16 — Same trade, different outcome

Learning intention: We are learning how economic, political and environmental influences shape what is traded, where it goes and what it costs.

Lesson outline

  1. Starter: “A cyclone hits banana farms in Queensland. What happens to the price of bananas in shops — and why?” Let students reason it out, then reveal the real precedent: banana prices spiked sharply after Cyclone Yasi in 2011.
  2. Teach: the three influences that shape trade flows, two examples each — ECONOMIC (production costs and wages; exchange rates), POLITICAL (tariffs, trade agreements, sanctions), ENVIRONMENTAL (climate and natural disasters; distance and geography).
  3. Skill: model the cause → effect → outcome chain on the worked-example slide (Cyclone Yasi → supply falls → prices rise) before students build their own.
  4. Task: worksheet — sort 9 scenario statements, complete two chains from the scenario cards, then the judgement question.
  5. Exit ticket: three quick questions on the final slide.

Activity instructions

  • Task 1 answers: economic — statements 1, 5, 8; political — 2, 4, 7; environmental — 3, 6, 9.
  • Task 2 scenario cards use fictional countries and are labelled as scenarios for classroom use. Chain A is environmental (cyclone destroys vanilla farms); Chain B is political (trade agreement removes a tariff).
  • Task 3 has no single right answer — assess whether the two reasons use lesson vocabulary (tariff, exchange rate, drought, distance…).
  • Non-specialist tip: a “weak” Australian dollar sounds bad but is good news for exporters — overseas buyers get more for their money.

External links

  • Cyclone Yasi and banana prices — search phrase: Cyclone Yasi 2011 banana prices Australia
  • Australia’s trade agreements — search phrase: DFAT Australia free trade agreements
Christian worldview — Caretaking Earth (Proverbs 27:23–24): “Be sure you know the condition of your flocks…” — wise stewardship pays attention and anticipates change. Discussion: why is it wise, and kind, to plan for shocks?

Lesson 17 — Trade shock simulation: the Suez blockage

Learning intention: We are learning how one trade shock can ripple through interconnected trade flows and affect people in many different places.

Lesson outline

  1. Starter: image description on the slide — a 400-metre ship stuck sideways in a canal, a tiny digger beside it. “One ship. Six days. Billions of dollars. How?”
  2. Teach: recount the Ever Given (ran aground in the Suez Canal in March 2021, blocking it for six days; hundreds of ships queued; about 12% of global trade passes through the canal); ripple effects — delays, shortages, higher freight prices, factories pausing; short-term versus long-term impacts; who is affected — producers, consumers, environments (rerouting around Africa means more fuel and emissions).
  3. Simulation: five groups, one role card each from the worksheet — Australian electronics retailer, European car factory manager, Egyptian canal pilot, container ship crew member, online shopper.
  4. Report and consolidate: spokespersons report (30–45 seconds each) while the class completes the impact grid; then the written consolidation.
  5. Exit ticket: three quick questions on the final slide.

Activity instructions

  • Timing guide: 15 min teach, 5 min group reading, 8 min impact grid, 8 min spokesperson reports, 10 min writing.
  • The impact grid maps producers / consumers / environments against short term and long term. Most groups find short-term impacts easily — prompt for long-term ones with “what would you do differently next year?”
  • The written consolidation is the assessable piece: it must use the words interconnection and trade flow correctly.
  • If you have a projector, an image search for Ever Given Suez gives striking real photos — the described image on the slide means the lesson also works without one.

External links

  • News coverage of the blockage — search phrase: Ever Given Suez Canal blockage March 2021
  • Why the canal matters — search phrase: Suez Canal share of global trade
Christian worldview — Building Community (Galatians 6:2): “Carry each other’s burdens” — when trade breaks down, we discover how much we depend on one another. Discussion: whose burden could you help carry when things go wrong around you?

Lesson 18 — Macadamia data lab

Learning intention: We are learning to construct and interpret line graphs, column graphs and pie charts using data about Australia’s macadamia trade.

Lesson outline

  1. Starter: “This nut is a world traveller — born here.” Macadamias are native to Australia and are bush tucker of First Nations Peoples, valued long before they became an export industry (respectful, present-tense link back to Week 2).
  2. Teach: the macadamia industry as a mini case of trade influences — grown in northern NSW and Queensland (climate: environmental influence), exported worldwide, with Kenya a major and growing competitor helped by lower production costs (economic influence). Trees take several years before commercial harvest, so shocks and competition bite hard.
  3. Skill stations: model each graph type from the worked-example slides, then students complete the three stations in the Data Booklet.
  4. Synthesis: 4–5 sentences describing Australia’s macadamia trade using all three graphs, including one economic and one political or environmental influence.
  5. Exit ticket: three quick questions on the final slide.

Activity instructions

  • Equipment: sharp pencils, rulers, protractors. Run the stations as a 12-minute carousel or straight through in order.
  • Station 1 (line graph): ten years of production data (labelled “Sample data for classroom use”, realistic magnitude, about 40–56 thousand tonnes) with a drought dip in 2019 that students must spot and explain.
  • Station 2 (column graph): kernel sales by destination — watch the ranking trap: Australia (6) beats North America (5), so the correct rank is Asia, Europe, Australia, North America.
  • Station 3 (pie chart, L11 recap): snacks 60% = 216°, ingredients 30% = 108°, other 10% = 36°; degrees must total 360°. Students construct the chart with a protractor in the provided circle.
  • All data tables in the booklet are labelled “Sample data for classroom use” — the verifiable facts (native to Australia; Australia and South Africa among the largest producers; Kenya a growing competitor; several years to first commercial harvest) are stated in the background box.

External links

  • Australian Macadamia Society: australianmacadamias.org (if the link changes, search phrase: Australian Macadamia Society industry statistics)
  • Macadamia origins and bush tucker — search phrase: macadamia native Australia First Nations bush tucker
