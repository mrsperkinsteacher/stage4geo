Stage 4 Geography | Year 7 | Interconnections and Trade — Week 5

Week 5 — Trade Flows

The nature and spatial patterns of trade flows, ending in the unit’s fieldwork. Outcomes: GE4-PRI-01, GE4-TAP-01. Threads: Seeking Justice / Building Community.

Lesson 13 — Trade flow wall

Learning intention: We are learning what trade flows are and how to describe the spatial patterns of Australia’s trade.

Lesson outline

  1. Starter: pairs guess Australia’s top 3 exports and top 3 imports, then the reveal — exports: iron ore, coal, natural gas (plus education as a service export); imports: refined petroleum, cars, telecom equipment and computers (recent years, DFAT data — describe rankings, don’t quote figures).
  2. Teach: trade flow, export, import, trading partner; goods vs services; the three-lens pattern language — direction, volume, distance — with the worked example sentence on the slide.
  3. Teach: Australia’s major two-way partners in rank order: China (largest by far), then Japan, USA, South Korea.
  4. Main activity — trade flow wall: full teacher instructions on the slides (see below).
  5. Task: worksheet Tasks 1, 3 and 4 (sorting, mini map with weighted arrows, pattern questions).
  6. Exit ticket: three quick questions on the final slide.

Activity instructions — trade flow wall

  • You need: a large world map (wall map, or projected onto a whiteboard), string/wool and sticky tack (or markers), and the 12 cut-out country cards from worksheet Task 2.
  • Each pair receives one country card listing what that country buys from and sells to Australia (qualitative descriptions only). Pairs locate their country and run string (or draw an arrow) from Australia to it, reporting their two-way trade aloud.
  • Make sure China, Japan, USA and South Korea are allocated; give China’s pair the thickest wool. Then step back and read the wall: most strings fan north into Asia.
  • Class pattern questions are on the discussion slide: direction (north), volume (China thickest), distance (mostly near neighbours — the thin Europe strings are the exception), region (Asia) and two reasons.
  • Worksheet Task 3 repeats the idea on paper: a schematic region map where students draw six arrows with thicker arrows for bigger trade, using a flows table labelled “Sample data for classroom use”.

External links

  • DFAT trade statistics: dfat.gov.au (search phrase: DFAT trade and investment at a glance)
  • Search phrase for a visual hook: Australia’s top trading partners map
Christian worldview — building community (Philippians 4:15–16): Paul describes a two-way flow of “giving and receiving” — trade at its best is mutual provision. Discussion: can buying and selling be a way of loving your neighbour? When might it stop being that?

Lesson 14 — The box that changed the world

Learning intention: We are learning how the shipping container changed world trade, and where the world’s main sea routes and chokepoints are.

Lesson outline

  1. Starter: “Everything in this room travelled in a box. Prove me wrong.” — a two-minute hunt for anything that never saw a shipping container (count parts, packaging and raw materials; almost everything fails the test).
  2. Teach: before containers (break-bulk loading, ships in port for a week or more, high cost) versus after (Malcolm McLean, 1956; standard 20 ft / 40 ft boxes; cranes; days in port). About 90% of world trade travels by sea (IMO/ICS figure).
  3. Watch: WSJ video How a Steel Box Changed the World (~4 minutes) — give students the “watch for a date, a name, a number” list first.
  4. Teach: major routes (Asia–Europe via Suez, trans-Pacific, via Panama) and the chokepoint concept — Suez, Panama, Malacca; the Ever Given blocked the Suez Canal for 6 days in March 2021.
  5. Skill: model the three-way summary (10–15 / 30–50 / 75–100 words) from the worked-example slide.
  6. Task: worksheet — three-way summary, Asia–Europe route mapping, chokepoints table. Exit ticket on the final slide.

Activity instructions

  • Task 2 works best with a printed world map for students to draw on; otherwise they sketch in the framed box on the worksheet. Route order: South China Sea → Strait of Malacca → Indian Ocean → Red Sea → Suez Canal → Mediterranean Sea.
  • Chokepoint answers: Suez connects the Red Sea and Mediterranean; Panama connects the Pacific and Atlantic; Malacca connects the Indian Ocean and South China Sea. They matter because many ships depend on one narrow passage.
  • Marking the summaries: headline = main idea only; paragraph = main idea + mechanism; detailed = adds evidence (a date, a number, a name) and a consequence.
  • If YouTube is blocked at your site, teach from the before/after slide and have students summarise that instead.

External links

  • Video (verified): How a Steel Box Changed the World — Wall Street Journal (~4 min)
  • Live ship-tracking map for a great 2-minute hook: search phrase MarineTraffic live map
  • Search phrase: Ever Given Suez Canal 2021 BBC
Christian worldview — the hidden workers (Colossians 3:23): about 1.9 million seafarers (ICS figure) spend months at sea, unseen, so our shelves stay full; ordinary invisible work has dignity when done “as for the Lord”. Discussion: who are the invisible people behind your belongings, and what would gratitude to them look like?

Lesson 15 — Fieldwork: the goods audit

Learning intention: We are conducting fieldwork: collecting, recording, graphing and interpreting real data about where our goods come from.

Lesson outline

  1. Starter: frame the inquiry question — “Where do the goods in our place come from?” — and have every student write a hypothesis in the booklet before touching anything (“I predict most goods will come from … because …”).
  2. Method demo: model one worked-example row live (pencil case → “Made in China” → Asia) before anyone moves.
  3. Fieldwork: run the four timed phases from the slide — 10 min collect (15 items: classroom, bags, canteen packaging, clothing labels) / 10 min tally by region / 15 min column graph / 10 min conclusions.
  4. Conclude: four scaffolded questions plus the limitations box; conclusions must quote numbers from the student’s own graph.
  5. Exit ticket and collect the booklets — they are the assessable product (evidence for GE4-TAP-01).

Activity instructions

  • Decide in advance which areas students may search, and collect some canteen packaging beforehand. Pairs work well: one handles items, one records.
  • Safety and ethics rules are in the booklet and on a slide: ask permission, handle items carefully and return them, stay in approved areas, record objects not people. Ethics is part of the method, not just behaviour management.
  • Tricky labels: “Designed in California, assembled in China” = record China; “Made in Australia from imported ingredients” = record Australia (Oceania) but raise it in limitations. No label = “unknown”, choose another item.
  • Graph checklist is TALC: Title, Axes labelled, Level same-width columns, Colour. The booklet contains a pre-drawn 15-row graph grid.
  • Good limitations answers: only 15 items, only one room, unlabelled items skipped (a bias), and “Made in” shows final assembly only.

External links

  • Background on fieldwork in the NSW syllabus: curriculum.nsw.edu.au (search phrase: NESA Geography 11–24 fieldwork Stage 4)
Christian worldview — gratitude (1 Thessalonians 5:18): every item recorded is the work of many hands in many places — farmer, factory worker, seafarer, driver. Discussion: pick one item from your data — how many workers in how many countries helped it reach you, and does knowing that change how you treat it?
